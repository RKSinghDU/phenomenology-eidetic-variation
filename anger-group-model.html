Eidetic Variation — Anger Group

Completed model · Phenomenology seminar activity · Step-by-step worked example
Step 1 — Five varied instances of anger
1
A colleague takes credit for your work in a team meeting, in front of your manager.
Intensity: high · Context: workplace · Target: a specific person · Moral dimension: strong
2
You read about a child being abused — someone you will never meet, in a country you have never visited.
Intensity: high · Context: abstract/news · Target: injustice itself · Moral dimension: very strong
3
A friend cancels plans for the third time without apology, again at the last minute.
Intensity: moderate · Context: personal relationship · Target: a person + a pattern · Moral dimension: present
4
You discover your salary is significantly lower than a less experienced colleague doing the same role.
Intensity: high · Context: institutional · Target: a system/policy · Moral dimension: strong
5
Alone at home, you realise you have been deceiving yourself about a relationship for years.
Intensity: moderate-high · Context: private, no witness · Target: oneself · Moral dimension: present but inward
Step 2 — Imaginative variation: essential or accidental?
Moral dimension — sense of wrongdoing or injusticeEssential
Remove it: can you be angry without any sense that something wrong has occurred?
Across all 5 instances, a perceived violation is present — even instance 5 involves self-betrayal as a form of wrong. When we imagined anger with no wrongdoing at all, it collapsed into frustration or distress. The moral dimension appears invariant.
Specific intentional target (a person or agent)Accidental
Remove it: can anger be directed at an abstraction — "the system", injustice itself?
Yes — instance 2 (news about child abuse) and instance 4 (pay inequality) show anger without a specific nameable agent. The target can be a situation, structure, or principle. A specific person is not essential.
Social witness — someone seeing the event or the angerAccidental
Remove it: can anger exist entirely privately, with no audience?
Instance 5 (alone at home, self-directed anger) demonstrates that anger requires no witness. Unlike shame, anger does not seem to require even an imagined observer. Witness is accidental.
High bodily intensity — heat, surge, physiological arousalContested
Remove it: can anger be cold, quiet, sustained — without the surge?
Our group was divided. Instance 3 and instance 4 can feel cold and slow-burning rather than hot. "Cold anger" is a recognisable experience. We classified intensity as accidental — but noted that some felt sense of activation (even if cold) may still be invariant. This remains open.
Impulse toward redress or changeEssential
Remove it: can anger exist with total indifference to change — no desire that things be different?
When we imagined this, the experience became more like grief or despair. Anger seems to carry within it an orientation toward the world being otherwise — a demand that the wrong be corrected, even if suppressed. This directedness toward change appears essential.
Duration — sustained over timeAccidental
Remove it: can anger be instantaneous — a flash — and still be anger?
Yes. A sudden flash of anger at an injustice is still anger. Duration varies widely across our instances. Accidental.
Step 3 — Eidetic structure of anger
Essential features
A perceived transgression, violation, or injustice — the moral dimension
An intentional object (even if abstract — a situation, system, principle)
An orientation toward redress — the world being otherwise
Some felt activation (even if cold or suppressed)
Accidental features
A specific named person as the target
Social witness or audience
High physiological intensity or heat
Extended duration
Outward expression or action
Group's one-sentence eidetic definition:
Anger is the experience of a perceived moral violation — directed at a person, system, or situation — that carries within it a demand that the world be otherwise, regardless of whether that demand is expressed or witnessed.
Step 4 — Productive tensions and debrief questions
Where did our analysis produce genuine disagreement?
Bodily intensity was contested. "Cold anger" challenged the assumption that anger is always a hot surge — and this productively opens the question of whether felt activation (however low) is always present, or whether anger can be purely cognitive-evaluative.
What did this reveal about eidetic method?
Disagreements don't signal failure — they signal where more rigorous variation is needed. We didn't need a survey. We needed more varied instances and more disciplined imagination. This is Husserl's point: eidetic knowledge is a priori, but it requires careful work.
How does this boundary matter clinically?
If a client says "I feel angry" — our eidetic structure suggests we should explore: what is the perceived violation? What does the client want to be different? A client who cannot identify a violation or a demand may be experiencing something else — grief, despair, or frustration. The distinction guides the clinical response.
